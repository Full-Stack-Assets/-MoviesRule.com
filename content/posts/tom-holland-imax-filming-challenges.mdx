Tom Holland, famed for his role as Spider‑Man, recently sat down with historian Tom Holland for a Business Insider interview about Christopher Nolan’s upcoming IMAX epic “The Odyssey”. He walked through the physical and logistical hurdles that come with shooting in the giant‑format camera system. Understanding these challenges matters for anyone following high‑budget filmmaking, as they shape everything from scheduling to visual storytelling.

<Callout type="takeaway">IMAX’s unrivaled visual impact comes with technical and logistical hurdles that shape every stage of production.</Callout>

## What happened
The interview, published on July 14, 2026, revealed that the actor‑turned‑interviewee emphasized Nolan’s commitment to shooting “The Odyssey” entirely in native IMAX, a choice that drives both prestige and complexity.

He noted that the current IMAX MKII cameras weigh roughly 30 pounds and are limited to about 30 minutes of continuous recording, forcing crews to plan shots meticulously and often rely on rigs rather than handheld movement.

Because the larger sensor requires significantly more illumination, sets must be lit more brightly, which can increase both on‑set costs and post‑production color‑grading work.

## Why it matters
The constraints of IMAX shape budgeting, scheduling, and creative decisions. Studios must weigh the premium ticket revenue against higher equipment, lighting, and labor costs, while directors adapt their visual language to the format’s limitations. For audiences, the trade‑off determines whether the immersive experience justifies longer production timelines and potentially higher ticket prices.

## How to think about it
Filmmakers considering IMAX should start with a clear visual plan, allocate extra budget for lighting, and schedule additional days for camera setups. Pre‑visualization helps decide which sequences truly benefit from the format, while a hybrid approach—mixing IMAX with standard cameras—can balance impact and cost.
