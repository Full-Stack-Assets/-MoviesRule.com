Today Iowa remains under a stretch of heat and humidity, with afternoon highs just under 90°F. Tomorrow the temperature climbs into the mid‑90s, pushing heat indices toward 100‑105°F. Meanwhile, the forecast calls for two rounds of storms, the second targeting northeast Iowa with a level‑3 out of 5 severe weather risk. Residents should watch for damaging winds, hail, and a low‑chance tornado threat. The evolving pattern will shift to cooler 80s after a cold front arrives on Tuesday.

<Callout type="takeaway">Iowa faces a hot weekend and a heightened severe‑storm risk in the northeast.</Callout>

## What happened

Today the sky is mostly sunny with a high of 89°F and light southeast winds. A brief chance of early‑morning storms exists in northern Iowa, but rain chances remain low for the rest of the day.

Tomorrow brings the most hot and humid day of the week, with highs in the mid‑90s and heat indices climbing to 100‑105°F. An evening storm system is expected to move across northeast Iowa, carrying a level‑3 severe weather risk that includes damaging winds and hail; tornadoes remain unlikely.

By Tuesday a cold front will push temperatures back into the 80s, providing relief for the work week and ending the immediate heat wave.

## Why it matters

The extreme heat raises the risk of heat‑related illnesses, especially for outdoor workers and vulnerable populations. The severe‑storm threat in northeast Iowa could disrupt travel, damage property, and impact agriculture just as crops are maturing. Additionally, smoke from Canadian wildfires may add haze, reducing air quality across the state.

## How to think about it

Treat the weekend as a two‑phase event: prioritize heat safety on Saturday and Sunday—stay hydrated, limit strenuous activity, and use air‑conditioned spaces. As evening approaches, shift focus to storm preparedness: keep windows closed, secure loose objects, and have a battery‑powered weather radio or phone alerts enabled. Starting Monday, plan for cooler conditions and consider any damage assessments from the storms.

<Callout type="warning">Do not ignore heat‑index warnings; prolonged exposure above 100°F can quickly lead to heat exhaustion or heat stroke.</Callout>
